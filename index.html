Is Your LLM Overcharging You?

When you use an AI model, the more work it does, the more you pay. But how do you know how much work the model really did?

Ander Artola Velasco1 · Stratis Tsirtsis2 · Nastaran Okati3 · Manuel Gomez Rodriguez1
1MPI for Software Systems · 2Hasso Plattner Institute · 3MPI for Intelligent Systems
Read the paper Code & experiments

Tokens, the currency of modern AI

From simple chatbots like ChatGPT to advanced coding agents like Claude Code, most AI models essentially do the same thing: they generate text that answers your request. To build their answers, they use small chunks of text called tokens.

The more tokens in the response, the more computational work the model needs to do behind the scenes. And AI computations are heavy! It would be extremely slow to run the best models on a regular computer. For this reason, when you submit your request, it is sent over the internet to a more powerful computer owned by an AI provider. The model — which lives on their computer — generates the answer token by token, and the provider sends the final answer back to you.

What is the oldest city in the world? The oldest city is Damascus.

Naturally, the provider wants to get compensated for the computations done by the model to answer your request, since they have to pay for equipment and electricity to run the model. Providers currently do this following a simple pay-per-token billing. The more tokens required to fulfill your request, the more computations, the more money you pay.

Even in those cases, you have certain limits to how much you can use the model — for example, Claude has weekly quotas that get larger as you transition from free to paid plans and increase even further as you pay for a higher tier. In the background, the provider determines whether you reached your quota or not based on how many tokens the model has generated for you. In enterprise, actual bills based on the number of tokens are much more common compared to free or monthly subscription plans.

The same answer, different prices

Every extra token raises the bill — even though the text you receive never changes.

As AI-as-a-service becomes ever more prevalent, with a whole industry forming around it, we ask the following natural question:

We ask:

Can tokenizations be manipulated to overcharge users by inflating the token count of an AI's answers?

Can tokenizations be manipulated?

Unfortunately, the answer is yes. We present a procedure a malicious provider could use to inflate the tokenizations they report. As the figure below shows, this procedure takes the tokenization the AI produced, picks a token, and splits it into two smaller ones — raising the total count without changing a single character — then repeats this a chosen number of times, m.2 Worse still, we show that most tokenizations found this way could have been generated by the AI on its own, so they look perfectly plausible to the user.

The figures below show experiments with a real AI (Llama-3.2-1B, an LLM). The first shows how many extra tokens a malicious provider could add by applying the procedure; the second translates those extra tokens into the provider's profit. As you can see, the overcharge can be substantial. The different colors represent how varied the tokens used by the AI are, with the blue lines correspond to more varied tokenizations, which let a malicious provider overcharge more without raising the user's suspicion.

Takeaway

Tokenizations can be manipulated to overcharge users: splitting tokens inflates the count without changing a single character of the text.

Pay per character, not per token

If tokenizations can be manipulated while users pay by the token, is there a better way to price AI — one that leaves nothing to manipulate? There is:3

We show that

The only way to price AI answers that removes any incentive for a provider to overcharge is to bill by the character.

The intuition is simple: "Damascus" is eight characters no matter how many tokens the provider claims the model used. A character count can't be inflated by re-tokenizing the text, so there is nothing left to game!

Pay-per-token

The price depends on how the text is encoded, which is a (partly) random process and can be manipulated.

Pay-per-character

The price depends only on the text itself. However you encode it, the count is fixed — there is nothing left to game, removing the incentive to manipulate tokenizations.
